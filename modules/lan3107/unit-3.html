What is cultural etiquette?

Cultural etiquette refers to the accepted norms of behaviour within a given culture. In professional settings, awareness of cultural differences shows respect and helps build strong working relationships with international colleagues and clients. What is considered polite in one culture may be inappropriate in another, so understanding these differences is an essential professional skill.

Greetings and physical contact

The way people greet one another varies significantly around the world.

Region / cultureTypical greeting
East Asia (Japan, Korea)A bow — the deeper the bow, the more formal the greeting.
Hong Kong, SingaporeA firm handshake is standard in professional settings.
Western Europe, North AmericaA firm handshake; in some countries, a light kiss on the cheek between acquaintances.
Middle EastA handshake between men; wait for the other person to initiate if greeting someone of the opposite gender.
South AsiaThe namaste (palms pressed together, slight bow) is common in India and Nepal.

Eye contact

  • In many Western cultures, sustained eye contact signals confidence and honesty.
  • In some East Asian and African cultures, prolonged direct eye contact can be seen as disrespectful or confrontational, particularly with someone of higher status.
  • A safe approach in any cross-cultural setting is to mirror the other person's level of eye contact.

Punctuality

  • In many Northern European and East Asian professional cultures, punctuality is a sign of respect. Arriving late — even by a few minutes — can create a poor impression.
  • In some Latin American and Middle Eastern cultures, schedules can be more flexible, and a slight delay is not considered rude.
  • When in doubt, it is always safer to arrive on time or slightly early.

Communication styles

Cultures can be broadly described as either direct or indirect in their communication style.

Direct communicatorsIndirect communicators
  • State views and opinions plainly.
  • Value clarity and straightforwardness.
  • Examples: Germany, the Netherlands, Australia, the United States.
  • Use subtle language to avoid causing offence or embarrassment.
  • 'That might be difficult' may mean 'no'.
  • Examples: Japan, China, Korea, many Middle Eastern cultures.

Key point

Misunderstandings often arise when a direct communicator interprets an indirect refusal as a 'yes'. When working across cultures, pay attention to tone, body language, and context, not just the literal words.

Business card etiquette

In many Asian business cultures, exchanging business cards is a formal ritual that signals respect.

✓ Do

  • Present and receive business cards with both hands.
  • Take a moment to look at the card carefully before setting it down respectfully on the table.
  • Have cards printed in both English and Chinese when working in Hong Kong.
  • Carry your cards in a card holder to keep them in good condition.

✗ Don't

  • Write on someone's business card.
  • Shove a card into your pocket straight away.
  • Place anything on top of the card while it is on the table.
  • Hand out cards with one hand, or with the card facing away from the recipient.

Sharing Hong Kong culture with colleagues

When working alongside international colleagues, you may be asked to share aspects of Hong Kong culture. A useful structure is: introduce the topic → give a brief description → share your personal connection.

Template

'One thing that is quite special about Hong Kong is [topic]. [Brief description]. Personally, [your connection or opinion].'

Food culture

Dim sum (點心) is a centrepiece of Hong Kong's food culture. Families and colleagues visit Chinese restaurants — particularly on weekend mornings — for a shared meal of steamed dumplings, rice rolls, and pastries served in bamboo steamers. The tradition of eating together and sharing dishes reflects Hong Kong's strong sense of community.

Festivals and traditions

FestivalKey customs
Lunar New YearLion dances, fireworks, and the exchange of red packets (利是, laisee) containing money as a symbol of good luck.
Mid-Autumn FestivalFamilies and friends gather to admire the full moon, eat mooncakes, and carry lanterns.
Dragon Boat FestivalTeams race long wooden boats to the beat of a drum, commemorating the legend of the poet Qu Yuan.

Places of interest

  • Victoria Peak — Offers panoramic views of Hong Kong's skyline, harbour, and surrounding islands.
  • The Star Ferry — A historic passenger ferry crossing Victoria Harbour, one of Hong Kong's most iconic experiences.
  • The Big Buddha (天壇大佛) — A large bronze statue on Lantau Island, set within a serene monastery complex.
  • Temple Street Night Market — A vibrant street market known for street food, fortune tellers, and local goods.

Work culture and values

Hong Kong's professional culture is known for its efficiency, fast pace, and strong work ethic. Key values include:

  • Hierarchy and respect for seniority — In most workplaces, decisions flow from senior to junior. Address senior colleagues respectfully using their titles.
  • Relationships (關係, guanxi) — Building and maintaining good personal relationships is important in business dealings.
  • Face (面子, miànzi) — Avoiding actions that might embarrass or humiliate others publicly is a key social principle.

Useful expressions

Introducing a cultural topic

  • 'One thing that is quite unique about Hong Kong is …'
  • 'Something that might surprise you about our culture is …'
  • 'In Hong Kong, it is quite common to …'
  • 'Unlike in some countries, here we tend to …'

Asking about someone's culture

  • 'What are some of the most important traditions in your country?'
  • 'How do you usually celebrate [festival] back home?'
  • 'Is there anything you find different about the culture here compared to where you're from?'

Responding with interest

  • 'That's fascinating — I didn't know that.'
  • 'It sounds quite similar to / different from what we do here.'
  • 'I'd love to learn more about that.'

Useful vocabulary

Word / phraseMeaning
etiquetteThe accepted code of polite and courteous behaviour within a society or profession.
customA traditional way of behaving that is specific to a particular culture or community.
normA standard or typical pattern of behaviour that is accepted within a group.
valueA principle or belief that guides a person's or culture's behaviour.
hierarchyA system in which people or things are arranged according to their level of importance or authority.
gestureA movement of the body, especially the hands, used to express a feeling or idea.
tabooA social or cultural rule that forbids a particular topic or action.
traditionA long-established custom or practice passed down through generations.
bilingualAble to use two languages fluently.
diverseIncluding a wide variety of backgrounds, perspectives, or characteristics.
interculturalRelating to or involving different cultures interacting with one another.
