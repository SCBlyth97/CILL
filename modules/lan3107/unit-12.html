Meeting expressions — full reference

The following is a complete reference of expressions for each stage of a professional meeting. Use this as a revision tool to ensure you have a varied and appropriate range of language available.

Opening a meeting

  • 'Good [morning/afternoon], everyone. Thank you for joining us today.'
  • 'Let's get started. Welcome to today's meeting.'
  • 'The purpose of today's meeting is to [discuss / decide on / review] …'
  • 'We have [X] items on the agenda. Let's start with the first one.'
  • 'Could we begin with a quick introduction from each person?'

Asking for and giving opinions

  • 'What are your thoughts on …?'
  • 'How do you feel about this proposal?'
  • 'Does anyone have a view on …?'
  • 'In my opinion, …'
  • 'From my point of view, …'
  • 'I strongly feel that …'
  • 'As I see it, …'

Making suggestions

  • 'I suggest that we …'
  • 'What if we …?'
  • 'Why don't we …?'
  • 'One option could be to …'
  • 'It might be worth considering …'
  • 'I'd like to propose that we …'

Agreeing

  • 'I completely agree.'
  • 'Absolutely. That's a great point.'
  • 'I think that's an excellent suggestion.'
  • 'You're right. I couldn't agree more.'
  • 'That makes a lot of sense.'

Partially agreeing

  • 'I see your point, but I think we also need to consider …'
  • 'That's true to some extent. However, …'
  • 'I agree with part of that, though I'm not sure about …'
  • 'You make a good point. At the same time, …'

Disagreeing politely

  • 'I'm not sure I agree with that. My concern is …'
  • 'I see it a little differently. I think …'
  • 'I understand where you're coming from, but I feel that …'
  • 'With respect, I think there might be an issue with …'
  • 'I appreciate that perspective. At the same time, …'

Stating preferences

  • 'I prefer [A] to [B] because …'
  • 'I would rather [A] than [B].'
  • 'I find [A] more [adjective] than [B].'
  • 'Between the two options, I lean towards [A] because …'

Evaluating suggestions (using concessive language)

  • 'Although this approach has merits, I'm a little concerned about …'
  • 'Even though the idea sounds good, we should consider …'
  • 'Granted, … but …'
  • 'Despite the benefits, I think …'

Checking understanding

  • 'If I understand correctly, you're saying that …'
  • 'Could you clarify what you mean by …?'
  • 'Could you say that again, please?'
  • 'So your point is …? Is that right?'

Reaching a consensus

  • 'It seems like we're all in agreement on this.'
  • 'Can we agree that …?'
  • 'So the agreed approach is to …'
  • 'Are we all happy to go with that?'

Closing a meeting

  • 'To summarise, we have decided …'
  • 'The action points are as follows: …'
  • 'Our next meeting will be on [date].'
  • 'Thank you all for your contributions today.'
  • 'I'll circulate the minutes / action points by [deadline].'

Strategies for elaborating on opinions

In oral assessments and professional discussions, a bare opinion is never enough. Effective elaboration develops your point through reasons, examples, comparisons, and consequences. The following strategies can be combined to build a fully developed response.

Strategy 1: Give a reason

Explain why you hold your view.

Linking phrases

'This is because …', 'The main reason is that …', '… mainly because …'

Strategy 2: Give an example

Support your reason with a concrete illustration.

Linking phrases

'For example, …', 'For instance, …', 'To illustrate, …', 'A good example of this is …'

Strategy 3: Acknowledge the other side

Show balanced thinking by conceding a point before asserting your own.

Linking phrases

'Although …, I still think …', 'While I can see the advantages of …, I believe …', 'Granted, …, but …'

Strategy 4: Explain consequences or outcomes

Describe what will happen as a result of the proposal or action.

Linking phrases

'As a result, …', 'This would lead to …', 'Consequently, …', 'If we do this, we will …'

Strategy 5: Compare options

Use comparison to justify why your preferred option is better.

Linking phrases

'Compared with …, this option is more …', 'Unlike …, this approach would …', 'While [A] offers …, [B] is better suited to … because …'

Putting it all together

A strong elaborated opinion might use three or four of these strategies in sequence. Aim to speak for 30–45 seconds per point, moving naturally from claim → reason → example → consequence.

Example — fully elaborated opinion

'I prefer Option A — the digital marketing campaign — over Option B. [Reason] This is mainly because digital channels allow us to target specific audiences far more precisely than print advertising. [Example] For instance, on social media platforms, we can select exactly which age groups and interests we want to reach. [Contrast] Although print advertising has a broader reach, it is more difficult to measure its effectiveness. [Consequence] By choosing the digital campaign, we will be able to track engagement in real time and adjust our approach if needed.'

Assessment preparation checklist

Use this checklist to make sure you are ready for your oral assessment.

✓ Language

  • I can state, explain, and justify my opinions.
  • I can make and elaborate on suggestions.
  • I can agree, partially agree, and disagree politely.
  • I can state and compare preferences using varied language.
  • I can use concessive language ('although', 'while', 'granted').
  • I can check and confirm understanding.
  • I can help move a group towards a consensus.

✓ Communication skills

  • I listen actively and respond to what others say, not just what I planned to say.
  • I ask follow-up questions to keep the discussion going.
  • I build on others' points using phrases like 'Following on from what [name] said, …'
  • I maintain appropriate eye contact and positive body language.
  • I give others space to speak and do not dominate the discussion.

✓ Pronunciation

  • I stress the right syllable in key words (e.g. PRE·sent vs pre·SENT).
  • I use sentence stress to highlight key information.
  • I link words naturally in phrases (connected speech).
