What is small talk?

Small talk is casual conversation used to break the ice and establish connections. It involves non-work-related topics and helps build rapport, trust, and a positive atmosphere in social or professional settings. It is a crucial social skill that helps people connect, feel comfortable, and get to know each other better.

Suitable and unsuitable topics

Choosing the right topic is essential for successful small talk. Some topics are universally safe, while others can make people feel uncomfortable or create tension in a professional setting.

Suitable topicsUnsuitable topics
  • Weather
  • Travel and holidays
  • Food and drink
  • Movies and TV shows
  • Sports (e.g. a recent game or event)
  • Leisure, hobbies, and interests
  • Transport and commuting
  • Fashion and style
  • Pets and animals
  • Politics
  • Religion
  • Personal finances
  • Health issues
  • Personal relationships
  • Gossip

Active listening

In small talk, choosing a suitable topic is important, but listening actively is equally so. By giving appropriate responses as the other person speaks, you show engagement and help sustain the conversation.

Short responses to show interest

Use brief responses to signal that you are listening and encourage the other person to continue:

  • 'Wow.'
  • 'Well, …'
  • 'By the way, …'
  • 'That's great to hear.'
  • 'That sounds great.'
  • 'That sounds interesting.'
  • 'No problem.'

Small talk strategies

Three active listening strategies help keep a small talk conversation flowing:

  1. Using echo words
  2. Giving positive comments or compliments
  3. Asking follow-up questions and bringing up new topics

Strategy 1: Using echo words

Echo words show interest by repeating a keyword from what the other person has just said.

Examples

A: 'I'll be going to Disneyland during my annual leave.'
B: 'Disneyland? That sounds fun!'

A: 'I'm thinking about trying out photography as a new hobby.'
B: 'Photography? That sounds great.'

Strategy 2: Giving positive comments or compliments

Positive comments and compliments maintain a warm, encouraging atmosphere during small talk.

Examples

'Wow, I also love a good lasagna.'
'That's an excellent way to spend the weekend.'

Strategy 3: Asking follow-up questions and bringing up new topics

Questions let you follow up on something already said or introduce a fresh topic to keep the conversation going. There are three types of question you can use.

Echo questions

Echo questions respond to what the other person has said by showing interest and surprise. Form them by repeating the auxiliary verb, or by using do / did if there is no auxiliary verb.

Definition — auxiliary verb

An auxiliary verb (such as be, do, or have) is used alongside a main verb to show tense and to form questions and negatives.

FormationExamples
Repeating the auxiliary verb A: 'I am playing badminton tomorrow.' → B: 'Are you? That sounds fun!'
A: 'I have just visited Italy.' → B: 'Have you? That sounds great.'
Using do / did (no auxiliary verb) A: 'I love playing board games with friends.' → B: 'Do you? That sounds entertaining!'
A: 'I just started work here last week.' → B: 'Did you? Welcome aboard!'

Wh- questions

Wh- questions ask for information. They begin with what, when, where, who, whom, which, whose, why, or how.

Form

Wh- word + auxiliary verb (be, do, or have) + subject + main verb or adjective/noun

Wh- wordAuxiliarySubjectMain verbOther
Howdoyoulikethe dessert?
Whatdoesthe servicecover?—
Wheredidyougoon holiday?
Whenwillyouvisitthe museum again?

Whyarethese restaurants—so popular?

Yes/no questions

Yes/no questions can be answered with a simple 'yes' or 'no'. Form them by starting with an auxiliary verb followed by the subject and main verb.

AuxiliarySubjectMain verbOther

Haveyoutriedthe food in the new restaurant nearby?

Intonation for questions

Paying attention to intonation makes your questions sound more natural.

Question typeIntonationExamples
Yes/no questions Rising ↗ at the end 'Have you tried the dim sum at that famous restaurant in Central?'
'Did you do anything else at the weekend?'
Wh- questions and most other question types Falling ↘ at the end 'What kind of food did they serve?'
'Where did you go hiking?'
'How's your new commute to work going?'

Other tips for small talk

Universal conversation starter: 'How's the …?'

The 'How's the …?' format is a versatile way to open or continue a conversation by asking about someone's experiences or well-being.

  • 'How's your weekend?'
  • 'How's the food at that restaurant?'

Sharing something light-hearted or making a recommendation

You can share a light-hearted comment about yourself or recommend something on a topic of common interest to encourage the other person to do the same.

Giving short responses to show interest

Brief responses signal that you are listening and invite the other person to keep talking:

  • 'Hmm.'
  • 'Uh huh.'
  • 'Oh yeah?'
  • 'Really?'
  • 'Wow!'
  • 'That's great!'
  • 'That sounds amazing!'
  • 'That's impressive!'

Question tags

Question tags are short phrases added to the end of a statement to turn it into a question and invite agreement or engagement. The tag should have the opposite polarity (positive or negative) to the statement.

StatementTag
It's a nice day,isn't it?
You don't like coffee,do you?
It is faster than driving,isn't it?

Ending a conversation politely

When you are ready to end a casual conversation, use one or more of these approaches:

  • Suggest a future meeting or follow-up action — e.g. 'It was great talking to you. We should grab lunch sometime next week.'
  • Use a closing phrase — e.g. 'Well, it was great talking to you. I'll let you get back to your day.'
  • Mention other commitments — e.g. 'It was great catching up but I've got to run to my next meeting.'
  • Let the person know you enjoyed the conversation — e.g. 'It's been really nice talking to you, but I need to head out now.'

Small talk etiquette

Good etiquette makes you appear friendly and confident; poor etiquette can make you seem rude or disinterested.

✓ Do

  • Listen attentively and show genuine interest.
  • Maintain eye contact and positive body language.
  • Ask follow-up questions to keep the conversation going.
  • Give the other person space to speak.
  • Use positive, encouraging responses.

✗ Don't

  • Look at your phone while someone is talking.
  • Interrupt the other person.
  • Dominate the conversation by talking only about yourself.
  • Bring up sensitive or controversial topics.
  • Gossip about colleagues or other people.

Useful expressions

Greeting and self-introduction

  • 'Hi. I'm [name]. I'm a [job position] at [company name]. It's very nice to meet you!'
  • 'Hello. My name is … I am a [job position] at [company name].'
  • 'Hi. I'm [name]. I work in the … department. I am responsible for … It's lovely to meet you.'

Showing interest

  • 'Hmm.' / 'Uh huh.' / 'Oh yeah?' / 'Really?' / 'Wow!'
  • 'That's great!' / 'That sounds amazing!' / 'That's impressive!'

Ending the conversation

  • 'I'd love to chat longer, but I really must run. See you soon.'
  • 'Sorry, I've got to go now, but I hope to see you around.'
  • 'Sorry, I have to attend a meeting now. Let's meet for lunch next week though.'
  • 'It's been lovely to meet you. Hopefully, I'll see you around.'
  • 'It's nice to meet you too. Have a great day!'
