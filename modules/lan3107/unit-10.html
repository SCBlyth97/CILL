Stating preference

In professional discussions, you are often asked to choose between options and explain your choice. Using varied language to state preferences makes your contribution sound more natural and confident.

Core preference structures

StructureExample
I prefer [noun / -ing] to [noun / -ing]'I prefer online exhibitions to traditional formats.'
I prefer [noun] over [noun]'I prefer digital displays over printed guides.'
I would rather [base verb] than [base verb]'I would rather invest in interactive exhibits than in renovation.'
I find [noun / -ing] more [adjective] than …'I find interactive displays more engaging than static ones.'
[Option A] is more [adjective] than [Option B]'The digital tour is more cost-effective than a physical redesign.'
[Option A] is more likely to [verb]'This approach is more likely to attract younger visitors.'

Justifying a preference

Always support your preference with a reason. Use the C → R → E structure (Claim → Reason → Example) from Unit 5.

Example

'I prefer digital guides to printed brochures. [Reason] This is because they are easier to update and far more cost-effective in the long run. [Example] For instance, if we change an exhibition, we can update the digital guide instantly without printing costs.'

Comparing and contrasting

When evaluating two or more options, you need language to highlight both similarities and differences.

Describing differences

StructureExample
[A] is more [adjective] than [B]'Option A is more affordable than Option B.'
[A] is [adjective]-er than [B]'The first venue is larger than the second.'
Unlike [B], [A] …'Unlike the traditional format, the interactive approach allows visitors to …'
While / Whereas [B] …, [A] …'While Option B requires more staff, Option A can run with minimal supervision.'
[A] differs from [B] in that …'The digital display differs from the printed version in that it can be updated in real time.'

Describing similarities

StructureExample
Both [A] and [B] …'Both options aim to increase visitor engagement.'
[A] and [B] share …'The two proposals share a focus on accessibility.'
Like [B], [A] also …'Like the first proposal, this one also targets younger audiences.'
[A] is similar to [B] in that …'This plan is similar to last year's approach in that it centres on partnerships.'

Evaluating suggestions

Evaluating a suggestion means weighing its strengths and weaknesses before accepting, modifying, or rejecting it. A balanced evaluation acknowledges both sides before reaching a conclusion.

Steps for evaluating a suggestion

  1. Acknowledge the positive aspects.
  2. Identify any concerns or limitations.
  3. State your overall assessment or recommendation.

Example — balanced evaluation

'I think [name]'s suggestion of hosting a pop-up exhibition is interesting. [Strength] It would certainly create a lot of buzz and attract new visitors. [Concern] Although, I do wonder whether the logistics and cost of setting up temporary exhibits would be too high for our current budget. [Overall] Overall, I think it could work if we scaled it down slightly.'

Using 'although' and concessive language

Concessive language acknowledges one side of an argument while asserting the other. It is essential for nuanced, professional discussion.

Positioning 'although'

'Although' can appear at the beginning or in the middle of a sentence. Either position is correct, but the placement changes the sentence structure slightly.

PositionStructureExample
Beginning Although + [concession clause], [main clause]. 'Although this option is more expensive, it is likely to produce better results.'
Middle [Main clause], although [concession clause]. 'This option is likely to produce better results, although it is more expensive.'

Other concessive phrases

  • 'Even though this approach requires more time, …'
  • 'Despite the higher cost, I still feel that …'
  • 'While I can see the advantages of …, I think …'
  • 'It may be true that …, however …'
  • 'Granted, … but …'

Expressing disagreement tactfully

In professional group discussions, disagreement is natural and healthy — but how you express it matters. Tactful disagreement preserves relationships and keeps the discussion productive.

Key principle

Acknowledge the other person's point before presenting your own view. This shows respect and signals that you have listened, not just waited for your turn to speak.

Softening disagreement

  • 'I see your point, but I'm not entirely convinced that …'
  • 'That's an interesting idea. I'm a little concerned, though, about …'
  • 'I take your point. However, I wonder whether …'
  • 'I understand where you're coming from, but I feel that …'
  • 'I appreciate that perspective. At the same time, …'

Redirecting politely

  • 'Could we perhaps explore an alternative approach?'
  • 'What if we combined elements of both ideas?'
  • 'I think we might need to reconsider … in light of …'

Reaching a consensus

After a discussion, a group needs to move from different viewpoints towards a shared decision. Signalling that a consensus has been reached — or checking for agreement — is an important meeting skill.

Checking for agreement

  • 'Does everyone agree with that?'
  • 'Is everyone on board with this proposal?'
  • 'Are we all happy to go with option [A]?'
  • 'Can we agree that …?'

Signalling consensus

  • 'It seems like we're all in agreement on this.'
  • 'We've reached a consensus — we'll go with …'
  • 'I think we can all support this decision.'
  • 'So the agreed approach is to …'

Moving forward after a decision

  • 'Now that we've agreed on …, let's talk about the next steps.'
  • 'Who will be responsible for …?'
  • 'Let's set a deadline for …'

Useful expressions

Stating and comparing preferences

  • 'I prefer [A] because it is more [adjective] and better suited to …'
  • 'Between the two options, I would lean towards [A], mainly because …'
  • 'While both have their merits, I think [A] is more appropriate for our needs.'

Evaluating someone else's suggestion

  • 'I think that's a strong suggestion. One thing to consider, though, is …'
  • 'That could certainly work. I'd also want to make sure that …'
  • 'I can see the appeal of that idea, although I'm not sure it fits our timeline.'

Useful vocabulary

Word / phraseMeaning
preferenceA greater liking for one thing over another.
feasiblePossible and practical to do or achieve.
concessionAcknowledging the strength of an opposing point before asserting your own.
consensusA general agreement reached by all or most people in a group.
evaluateTo assess the strengths and weaknesses of something carefully.
tactfulCareful to avoid causing offence when communicating difficult views.
meritA quality or feature that deserves praise or consideration.
drawbackA disadvantage or problem associated with something.
viableCapable of working successfully; practicable.
compromiseAn agreement reached by each party giving up some of what they originally wanted.
