Promotional strategies

Promotion refers to the activities a company uses to communicate the value of its products or services to potential customers. There are many different promotional channels, and selecting the right mix depends on the target audience, budget, and goals.

Promotional methodDescriptionPossible benefit
Social media marketingPromoting via platforms such as Instagram, Facebook, or TikTok.Reaches a wide, younger audience; builds brand awareness quickly.
Influencer partnershipsCollaborating with online personalities to promote products.Builds trust and credibility through a familiar face.
Events and pop-upsHosting live or in-person experiences for customers.Creates an interactive and memorable experience.
Email / direct marketingSending personalised messages to an existing customer list.Targets customers who have already shown interest.
Print advertisingPosters, flyers, or newspaper/magazine advertisements.Reaches a broad range of potential customer types.
Word of mouth / referralsEncouraging satisfied customers to recommend the brand.Highly trusted and cost-effective.

Making and elaborating suggestions

When discussing promotional strategies in a meeting or group discussion, you will often need to make suggestions and then elaborate on them — explaining the reasoning or expected outcome.

Language for making suggestions

TypePhrases
Direct suggestion
  • 'I suggest that we …'
  • 'I recommend …'
  • 'We should consider …'
Tentative suggestion
  • 'What if we …?'
  • 'How about …?'
  • 'We could perhaps …'
  • 'It might be worth …'
Collaborative suggestion
  • 'Why don't we …?'
  • 'Shall we …?'
  • 'One option could be to …'

Elaborating on a suggestion

After making a suggestion, elaborate by explaining the goal, the method, and the expected outcome. Use linking phrases to connect your ideas.

Structure for elaborating

Suggestion → Reason / goal → How it works → Expected outcome

Example

'I suggest that we run a social media campaign on Instagram. [Goal] The aim would be to reach a younger audience who are active on the platform. [How] We could post short videos featuring the product in everyday situations. [Outcome] This would help get more people to know about us and could significantly boost our brand engagement.'

Linking phrases for elaboration

  • 'The reason for this is that …'
  • 'By doing this, we would be able to …'
  • 'This would help us to …'
  • 'As a result, …'
  • 'This is particularly effective because …'

Active listening in meetings

Active listening in a meeting goes beyond simply staying quiet while others speak. It involves showing genuine engagement, responding appropriately, and building on what others say.

Techniques for active listening in meetings

  • Nod and use brief acknowledgement phrases — 'Right.', 'I see.', 'Mm-hmm.'
  • Summarise or paraphrase — 'So what you're saying is …', 'If I understand correctly, …'
  • Ask follow-up questions — 'Could you tell us more about …?', 'What do you mean by …?'
  • Link your response to what was said — 'Building on what [name] just said, …'

Agreement and disagreement

In professional discussions, being able to agree, partially agree, and respectfully disagree is essential. The key is to acknowledge the other person's view before stating your own.

Expressing full agreement

  • 'I completely agree with that.'
  • 'That's a great point. I think so too.'
  • 'Absolutely. I couldn't agree more.'
  • 'You're right. That makes a lot of sense.'

Expressing partial agreement

  • 'I see your point, but I think we also need to consider …'
  • 'That's true to some extent. However, …'
  • 'I agree with part of that, though I'm not sure about …'
  • 'You make a good point. At the same time, …'

Expressing disagreement politely

  • 'I'm not sure I agree with that. My concern is …'
  • 'I see it a little differently. I think …'
  • 'I understand where you're coming from, but I feel that …'
  • 'With respect, I think there might be an issue with …'

Key point — polite disagreement

When you disagree in a professional setting, it is important to acknowledge the other person's view first ('I see your point…') before presenting an alternative ('…however, I think…'). This shows respect and keeps the discussion constructive.

Pronunciation: sentence stress

In spoken English, not all words in a sentence are given equal emphasis. Sentence stress is the pattern of which words receive more force or prominence. This helps the listener identify the key information.

Which words are typically stressed?

Usually stressed (content words)Usually unstressed (function words)
  • Nouns: campaign, product, team
  • Main verbs: suggest, launch, increase
  • Adjectives: new, effective, young
  • Adverbs: quickly, significantly, really
  • Articles: a, an, the
  • Prepositions: in, on, at, for
  • Pronouns: we, they, it
  • Auxiliary verbs: is, are, have, would

Using stress to highlight key information

You can shift the stress in a sentence to change the focus or meaning. Compare:

Example

'I SUGGEST we use social media.' (making a suggestion, not just mentioning it)
'I suggest we use SOCIAL MEDIA.' (emphasising which channel, not another method)
'I suggest WE use social media.' (emphasising who — not someone else)

Useful expressions

Introducing a promotion idea

  • 'One approach we could take is …'
  • 'Something that might work well is …'
  • 'I'd like to propose that we …'

Responding to a suggestion

  • 'That sounds promising. Could you tell us more about how it would work?'
  • 'I think that could be very effective, especially if we …'
  • 'I'm a little hesitant about that because …'

Useful vocabulary

Word / phraseMeaning

campaignA planned set of activities carried out over a period to achieve a goal.
target audienceThe specific group of people a product or message is aimed at.

engagementThe level of interaction or interest shown by an audience.
reachThe total number of people exposed to a promotional message.

credibilityThe quality of being trusted and believed.
