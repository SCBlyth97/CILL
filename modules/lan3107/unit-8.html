What is persuasion?

Persuasion is the act of convincing someone to adopt a particular belief, take a specific action, or change their mind. Effective persuasive communication in a professional context is built on clear reasoning, credible evidence, and respectful engagement — not pressure or manipulation.

Three classical appeals (Aristotle)

Logos — appealing to logic and reason through facts and evidence.
Ethos — appealing to the speaker's credibility and character.
Pathos — appealing to the audience's emotions and values.

Persuasive techniques

Persuasive speakers and writers use a range of techniques to make their arguments compelling.

TechniqueDescriptionExample
Facts and statistics Using data to support a claim. 'Studies show that 70% of customers make purchasing decisions based on online reviews.'
Expert opinion Citing an authority to add credibility. 'According to leading marketing experts, social media campaigns are most effective when …'
Rhetorical questions Asking a question that the audience is expected to agree with (no answer needed). 'Who wouldn't want to improve their company's brand image?'
Rule of three Listing three related points for impact and memorability. 'This approach is fast, affordable, and effective.'
Repetition Repeating a key word or phrase to emphasise it. 'This matters for our customers, for our brand, and for our future.'
Personal anecdote Sharing a relevant personal experience to build connection. 'When I first tried this method, I was surprised by how quickly the results came.'

Emphatic language

Emphatic language adds force and conviction to your statements. It signals to the listener that a particular point is especially important.

Emphatic adverbs and intensifiers

  • 'This is absolutely essential.'
  • 'The results were remarkably positive.'
  • 'I strongly believe that …'
  • 'This is by far the most effective approach.'
  • 'It is crucial that we …'

Emphatic 'do' / 'does' / 'did'

Using the auxiliary verb do, does, or did before a main verb in an affirmative sentence adds strong emphasis.

Examples

'This approach does produce results.'
'We did consider all the options carefully.'
'I do think this is worth pursuing.'

Cleft sentences with 'what'

A cleft sentence restructures a sentence to emphasise one element.

Structure and examples

What + subject + verb + be + [emphasised element]

'What we need is a clearer strategy.'
'What I suggest is that we expand online first.'

First conditionals

First conditional sentences describe a real or possible situation and its likely result. They are especially useful in persuasion because they allow you to explain the consequences of a course of action.

Structure

If + present simple → will + base verb
(condition) → (result)

Condition (If clause)Result (Main clause)
If we launch the campaign in October,we will capture the festive shopping market.
If we increase the marketing budget,our reach will expand significantly.
If we do not address this issue,it will affect customer satisfaction.

Variations

  • Unless (= if not): 'Unless we act now, we will miss the opportunity.'
  • As long as / provided that: 'As long as we stay within budget, the plan will work.'
  • In the event that (more formal): 'In the event that the proposal is approved, we will begin immediately.'

Interaction strategies

Interaction strategies are verbal techniques that help you manage a spoken exchange — keeping the conversation on track, seeking clarification, and responding to challenges.

Buying time to think

  • 'That's a good question. Let me think about that for a moment.'
  • 'I'd like to take a second to consider that.'
  • 'That's an interesting point. To answer that, …'

Clarifying your point

  • 'What I mean is …'
  • 'To put it another way, …'
  • 'In other words, …'
  • 'Let me rephrase that.'

Handling challenges or objections

  • 'That's a fair concern. I would argue, however, that …'
  • 'I understand your hesitation. Let me address that by …'
  • 'That's a valid point. At the same time, we should also consider …'

Pronunciation: word stress

As covered in Unit 1, word stress is the extra force given to one syllable when saying a word. Getting word stress right makes your speech clearer and easier to understand.

Stress in two-syllable words — nouns vs verbs

Many two-syllable words in English have the same spelling but different pronunciation depending on whether they are used as a noun or a verb. The noun stresses the first syllable; the verb stresses the second.

WordAs a nounAs a verb
recordRE·cord — 'Keep a REcord.'re·CORD — 'Please re·CORD the meeting.'
permitPER·mit — 'You need a PER·mit.'per·MIT — 'I will per·MIT this.'
presentPRE·sent — 'Give a PRE·sent.'pre·SENT — 'She will pre·SENT the findings.'
protestPRO·test — 'Join the PRO·test.'pro·TEST — 'They will pro·TEST the decision.'
increaseIN·crease — 'A big IN·crease.'in·CREASE — 'Costs will in·CREASE.'

Useful vocabulary

Word / phraseMeaning
persuasionThe act of convincing someone to adopt a particular view or take a specific action.
argumentA reason or set of reasons given to support a view.
evidenceInformation used to support or prove a claim.
rhetoricThe art of using language effectively to persuade an audience.
rhetorical questionA question asked for effect, not expecting a literal answer.
objectionA reason put forward against a proposal or view.
counter-argumentAn argument that challenges or responds to an opposing view.
anecdoteA short personal story used to illustrate a point.
emphaticExpressing something with special force or clarity to give it extra importance.
conditionalA sentence structure expressing a condition and its likely result.
