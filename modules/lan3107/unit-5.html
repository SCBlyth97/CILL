Expressing opinions

Being able to share your views clearly and respectfully is an essential skill in any professional setting. Opinions are most effective when stated confidently but politely, leaving room for dialogue.

Phrases for stating an opinion

StrengthPhrases
Strong / confident
  • 'I strongly believe that …'
  • 'I am convinced that …'
  • 'It is clear to me that …'
Neutral / balanced
  • 'In my opinion, …'
  • 'I think / feel that …'
  • 'From my point of view, …'
  • 'As I see it, …'
  • 'It seems to me that …'
Tentative / hedged
  • 'I would say that …'
  • 'I tend to think that …'
  • 'Perhaps …'

Inviting someone's opinion

  • 'What do you think about …?'
  • 'What is your view on …?'
  • 'How do you feel about …?'
  • 'Do you agree that …?'
  • 'Would you say that …?'

Explaining and supporting opinions

A well-developed opinion includes a claim (what you think), a reason (why you think it), and ideally an example to support it.

Structure: C → R → E

Claim — state your opinion. Reason — explain why. Example — give a specific example to support your point.

Example

'In my opinion, flexible working hours improve employee productivity. [Reason] This is because employees can work at times when they feel most focused and energetic. [Example] For instance, some people are most productive in the early morning, while others perform better later in the day.'

Linking phrases for giving reasons

  • 'This is because …'
  • 'The reason for this is that …'
  • '… mainly because …'

Linking phrases for giving examples

  • 'For example, …'
  • 'For instance, …'
  • 'To illustrate, …'
  • 'A good example of this is …'

Pronunciation: connected speech

In natural spoken English, words within a phrase are often linked together smoothly rather than pronounced separately. This is called connected speech. Understanding it makes listening easier and using it makes your speech sound more fluent.

Linking: consonant to vowel

When a word ends in a consonant sound and the next word begins with a vowel sound, the two words blend together.

Examples

'pick it up' → sounds like 'pi-ki-tup'
'an office' → sounds like 'a-nof-fice'
'turn it on' → sounds like 'tur-ni-ton'

Weak forms

Short, common words such as a, an, the, and, to, of, and for are often pronounced in a weak, reduced form in natural speech. The vowel in these words reduces to a schwa (/ə/) sound.

Examples

'a meeting' → a sounds like /ə/ ('uh')
'and the team' → and sounds like 'un' and the sounds like /ðə/
'for example' → for sounds like /fə/

Elision

Sometimes sounds are dropped altogether in fast, natural speech. This is called elision.

Examples

'next week' → the /t/ in 'next' is often dropped → sounds like 'nex week'
'last month' → the /t/ may be dropped → 'las month'

Listening tip

When you hear native or fluent speakers and cannot make out individual words, connected speech is often the reason. Train your ear by listening carefully to where words join together.

Workplace policies and benefits

Companies offer various policies and benefits to support their employees. Being able to describe and discuss these is useful in professional conversations.

Common workplace policies

PolicyDescription
Flexible working hoursEmployees can choose their start and finish times within agreed limits.
Remote / hybrid workingEmployees can work from home some or all of the time.
Leave policiesRules about annual leave, sick leave, and special leave (e.g. study leave, maternity/paternity leave).
Equal opportunitiesThe company commits to treating all employees fairly regardless of background.
Code of conductA set of rules governing professional behaviour and ethical standards in the workplace.

Common employee benefits

  • Medical and dental insurance
  • Staff discounts
  • Training and professional development programmes
  • Performance bonuses
  • Gym or wellness subsidies
  • Meal allowances or staff canteen

Discussing policies and benefits

Describing a policy

'Our company offers flexible working hours. In my opinion, this is very beneficial because it helps employees maintain a good work-life balance. For example, staff who have long commutes can avoid peak-hour traffic by adjusting their schedule.'

Useful expressions

Expressing opinions on workplace topics

  • 'I think flexible working hours are important because …'
  • 'From my point of view, providing staff training …'
  • 'I feel strongly that a positive work environment …'

Responding to someone's opinion

  • 'That's a good point. I also think that …'
  • 'I see what you mean, but I would argue that …'
  • 'I hadn't thought of it that way. Could you elaborate on that?'
  • 'I partially agree. However, …'

Useful vocabulary

Word / phraseMeaning
facilityA service or resource provided for a specific purpose (e.g. a gym, a canteen, a meeting room).
policyA set of rules or guidelines adopted by an organisation.
benefitAn advantage or reward provided to employees in addition to their salary.
welfareThe health, happiness, and overall well-being of a person or group.
productivityThe efficiency with which work is carried out.
moraleThe level of confidence and enthusiasm among a group of people.
absenteeismThe practice of frequently being absent from work without good reason.
turnoverThe rate at which employees leave a company and are replaced.
collocationA natural combination of words that frequently appear together.
schwaThe most common vowel sound in English (/ə/), used in unstressed syllables.
