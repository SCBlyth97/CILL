What is ESG?

ESG stands for Environmental, Social, and Governance. It is a framework used to evaluate how an organisation manages its responsibilities in these three areas. Companies, investors, and consumers increasingly use ESG criteria to assess whether an organisation is operating sustainably and ethically.

PillarWhat it coversExamples
Environmental (E) How a company manages its impact on the natural environment. Reducing carbon emissions, managing waste, conserving energy and water, using renewable energy sources.
Social (S) How a company manages relationships with employees, suppliers, customers, and communities. Fair labour practices, diversity and inclusion, community investment, data privacy and security.
Governance (G) How a company is led and controlled — its internal structures, policies, and ethics. Board diversity, executive pay transparency, anti-corruption measures, shareholder rights.

ESG in the workplace

Promoting ESG in your own organisation means taking practical steps to address environmental and social issues at a day-to-day level. Employees at all levels can contribute to a company's ESG goals.

Why ESG matters

  • For the environment — Reducing resource use and waste helps combat climate change and protects natural ecosystems.
  • For employees — A company with strong social practices tends to have a more positive, inclusive workplace culture.
  • For business — Strong ESG performance can improve a company's reputation, attract investors, and build customer loyalty.

Pronunciation: decoding multisyllabic words

Many long, complex words in English — especially those related to ESG and environmental topics — can be broken down into smaller parts called morphemes: prefixes, root words, and suffixes. Understanding common prefixes helps you work out the meaning of unfamiliar words and pronounce them correctly.

Decoding strategy

When you encounter an unfamiliar long word, try these steps:

  1. Look for a prefix you recognise at the start of the word.
  2. Identify the root word in the middle.
  3. Look for a suffix at the end (e.g. -tion, -ity, -ment, -al).
  4. Put the parts together to guess the meaning, then check in context.

Example

Overconsumption = over- (too much) + consumption (the act of using resources). Meaning: using more resources than is sustainable.

Structure of a workplace meeting

Most formal workplace meetings follow a predictable structure. Understanding this structure helps you participate effectively.

StagePurposeKey language
Opening Welcome attendees, confirm the agenda, and clarify the objective. 'Welcome, everyone. Today we're here to discuss …', 'Let's start with the first item on the agenda.'
Discussion Work through agenda items, share views, and explore options. 'What are your thoughts on …?', 'Could you elaborate on that?', 'Building on what [name] said …'
Decision / action points Agree on decisions and assign responsibilities. 'So we've agreed that …', 'Who will be responsible for …?', 'The deadline for this is …'
Closing Summarise outcomes and confirm next steps. 'To summarise, we have decided …', 'Our next meeting will be on …', 'Thank you all for your contributions.'

Interaction strategy: checking understanding

In any meeting or discussion, it is important to confirm that you have understood what has been said — and to verify that others have understood your own contributions.

Checking that you have understood

  • 'If I understand correctly, you're saying that …'
  • 'So your point is that … — is that right?'
  • 'Could you clarify what you mean by …?'
  • 'Could you repeat that, please? I want to make sure I have it right.'
  • 'I'm not sure I quite follow. Could you say that again?'

Checking that others have understood you

  • 'Does that make sense?'
  • 'Is that clear?'
  • 'Are there any questions about what I've just explained?'
  • 'Let me know if you'd like me to expand on any of that.'

Suggesting green measures

When presenting ESG suggestions in a professional discussion, combine the suggestion language from Unit 7 with specific, feasible ideas. Always link each measure to a clear environmental or social benefit.

Example — suggesting a green measure

'I suggest that we install energy-saving LED lighting throughout the office. This is because conventional lighting is one of the biggest contributors to our energy use. By switching to LEDs, we would significantly reduce our electricity consumption, which would lower both our costs and our carbon footprint.'

Common green workplace measures

  • Reducing single-use plastic in the office (e.g. replacing plastic cups with reusable ones).
  • Setting up a recycling programme for paper, glass, and electronic waste.
  • Encouraging employees to use public transport or cycle to work.
  • Reducing paper use by digitising processes and documents.
  • Installing motion-sensor lights and smart thermostats to reduce energy waste.
  • Sourcing sustainably produced supplies and materials.

Useful vocabulary

Word / phraseMeaning
ESGEnvironmental, Social, and Governance — a framework for assessing a company's ethical and sustainability performance.
sustainabilityThe ability to meet present needs without compromising the ability of future generations to meet theirs.
carbon footprintThe total amount of greenhouse gases produced, directly or indirectly, by an individual or organisation.
renewable energyEnergy from natural sources that are replenished continuously, such as solar, wind, or hydropower.
emissionThe release of gases (especially greenhouse gases) into the atmosphere.
ecosystemA community of living organisms interacting with their physical environment.
biodiversityThe variety of plant and animal life in a particular habitat or on Earth as a whole.
governanceThe way in which an organisation is directed, controlled, and held accountable.
transparentOpen and clear; not concealing information.
stakeholderA person or group with an interest in the success and activities of an organisation.
