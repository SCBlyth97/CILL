First day at work — dos and don'ts

A strong first impression sets the tone for all the working relationships that follow. Below are the key behaviours to aim for and to avoid.

✓ Do

  • Arrive on time.
  • Dress professionally.
  • Introduce yourself to colleagues.
  • Listen attentively.
  • Be polite, friendly, and enthusiastic.
  • Ask questions if you are unsure of anything.
  • Smile, make eye contact, and maintain a positive attitude.

✗ Don't

  • Be late.
  • Dress in unprofessional attire.
  • Be unprepared and seem disorganised.
  • Forget basic courtesies such as saying please and thank you.
  • Focus on your phone.
  • Ask personal questions.
  • Seem uninterested or distracted.

These are common words and terms you will encounter in a typical office environment.

Useful expressions

The following expressions cover the four key interaction types on a first day at work.

Greeting new colleagues

  • 'Hi! Nice to meet you.'

Introducing yourself

  • 'My name is [name].'
  • 'Hi, I'm [name].'

Responding to introductions

  • 'I'm very happy to be here.'
  • 'I'm glad to have the opportunity to work with such a great team.'
  • 'I'm excited to be here and I'm looking forward to working with you.'

Introducing others

  • 'May I introduce our new colleague, [name] to you?'
  • 'Let me introduce you to [name], our newest team member.'
  • 'Have you met [name], our new colleague?'

Ending a conversation politely

  • 'I'm afraid I have to get back to my work now, but it was nice meeting you.'
  • 'It was great talking with you, but I need to get back to my work now.'
  • 'I have a deadline to meet so I've got to run.'
  • 'I don't want to keep you from your other tasks, so I'll let you go for now.'

Pronunciation: syllables and word stress

Definition — syllable

A syllable is a unit of pronunciation. Every syllable contains a vowel (a, e, i, o, u). In most cases you can count the syllables of a word by counting its vowel sounds — for example, job has one syllable, or·der has two, and re·cep·tion has three.

Definition — word stress

Word stress is the extra force placed on one specific syllable when pronouncing a word. In most English words, only one syllable is stressed, and only vowels are stressed. For example: Or·der (stress on the first syllable) and re·CEP·tion (stress on the second syllable).

Word stress in two-syllable verbs

Key rule

Most two-syllable verbs have the stress on the second syllable. However, there are always exceptions to the rule.

PatternExamples
Stress on second syllable (typical) con·DUCT, as·SIST, re·STOCK, main·TAIN, per·FORM, cre·ATE, pre·PARE, pro·VIDE, pro·MOTE
Stress on first syllable (exceptions) PRO·cess, MAN·age, HAN·dle, CON·tact

Silent 'e' rule

When 'e' is the last letter in a word, it is usually silent (unpronounced) and it makes the vowel that comes before it sound like its name. For example, in create the final 'e' is silent and the 'a' before it is pronounced as the name of the letter 'a'.

Introducing yourself at work

When you meet new colleagues, a clear self-introduction covers three things: your name, your department, and your job duties.

Template

'Hi! Nice to meet you. My name is [name]. I'm from the [department] department. As a [job title], my job duties include [duties].'

Expressions for describing job duties

You can vary the way you describe what you do by using any of the following frames:

  • 'My job duties include …'
  • 'My main responsibilities are / include …'
  • 'My job involves …'
  • 'I'm responsible for …'
  • 'I'm tasked with …'

Example: 'As a customer service officer, I'm responsible for providing information to customers and responding to customer enquiries.'

Useful vocabulary

Verbs for describing job duties: assist, conduct, coordinate, handle, maintain, manage, organise, promote, provide, recruit.

Nouns commonly used in the workplace: benefit, colleague, conference room, cubicle, deadline, department, document, duty, position, responsibility.
