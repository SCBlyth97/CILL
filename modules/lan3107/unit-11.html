Making event arrangements

Making workplace arrangements involves confirming logistics — when, where, who, and what. Whether you are organising an internal meeting, a team event, or a corporate gathering, clear communication about the details prevents misunderstandings.

Language for making arrangements

PurposeExpressions
Proposing a time or date 'How does [day/time] work for you?', 'Would [date] be suitable?', 'I was thinking we could meet on …'
Confirming an arrangement 'That works for me.', 'Great, let's confirm that for [day] at [time].', 'I'll send a calendar invitation.'
Rescheduling 'I'm afraid I can't make it on [day]. Could we move it to …?', 'Would it be possible to push the meeting back to …?'
Confirming details 'Just to confirm — we're meeting at [time] in [location/platform].', 'Could you send through the dial-in details?'

Discussing responsibilities for an event

  • 'Who will be responsible for booking the venue?'
  • 'Could you take care of the catering?'
  • 'I'll handle the invitations if you can manage the equipment.'
  • 'Let's divide the tasks — [name] will look after …, and I'll be in charge of …'

Note-taking in meetings

Effective note-taking in meetings ensures that key decisions, action points, and deadlines are captured accurately. Good notes also serve as a reference after the meeting.

What to note

  • Decisions made — what was agreed.
  • Action points — who will do what, and by when.
  • Key discussion points — the main arguments or ideas raised.
  • Questions raised and answers given.

Tips for effective note-taking

✓ Do

  • Use abbreviations and symbols to write quickly (e.g. → for 'leads to', w/ for 'with', vs for 'versus').
  • Note the date, time, location, and names of attendees at the top.
  • Focus on key information rather than writing everything word for word.
  • Review and clarify your notes as soon as possible after the meeting.
  • Use headings and bullet points to organise information clearly.

✗ Don't

  • Try to transcribe everything — focus on substance, not every word.
  • Write in full sentences where a phrase will do.
  • Let note-taking distract you from actively participating.

Opening a meeting

The person chairing a meeting is responsible for getting it started in a structured way. A good opening sets a professional tone and ensures everyone knows the purpose and plan.

Opening sequence

  1. Welcome attendees — 'Good morning, everyone. Thank you for joining us today.'
  2. Introduce any new participants — 'I'd like to introduce [name], who is joining us from …'
  3. State the purpose — 'Today's meeting is to discuss / decide on / review …'
  4. Outline the agenda — 'We have three items on the agenda. First, … Second, … Finally, …'
  5. Set time expectations — 'We have about one hour, so let's make a start.'

Opening statement — example

'Good afternoon, everyone. Thank you all for coming. Today we're here to finalise the plans for our summer staff event. We have about forty-five minutes, so I'd like to cover three things: the venue, the budget, and the programme of activities. Let's get started with the first item — the venue.'

Managing the discussion

During a meeting, the chairperson guides the discussion, ensures everyone has the opportunity to contribute, and keeps the group on track.

Inviting contributions

  • 'What are your thoughts on this?'
  • '[Name], would you like to add anything?'
  • 'Does anyone have a different view?'

Keeping the discussion focused

  • 'Let's keep to the point for now.'
  • 'I think we should save that for later — let's focus on …'
  • 'We're getting a little off topic. Could we return to …?'

Moving on

  • 'Right, I think we've covered that point. Let's move on to the next item.'
  • 'Unless there are any other comments on this, shall we proceed to …?'

Closing a meeting

A well-managed close ensures that every participant leaves knowing what was decided, what they need to do next, and when they will meet again.

Closing sequence

  1. Summarise decisions — 'So, to summarise what we've agreed today …'
  2. Confirm action points — 'The action points are as follows: [name] will … by [date]; [name] will … by [date].'
  3. Confirm the next meeting — 'Our next meeting will be on [date] at [time].'
  4. Thank participants — 'Thank you all for your contributions today. Have a good day.'

Online meetings

Online meetings present their own communication challenges. The following expressions are specifically useful for virtual settings.

Technical checks

  • 'Can everyone see my screen?'
  • 'Sorry, I think I was on mute. Could I repeat that?'
  • 'I'm having some connection issues. Could you repeat that?'
  • 'Your audio is breaking up — could you try reconnecting?'

Managing turn-taking online

  • 'I'll ask everyone to mute their microphones while others are speaking.'
  • 'Please use the raise-hand function if you'd like to contribute.'
  • '[Name], I can see you'd like to say something — please go ahead.'
  • 'Let's go round the room — [name], would you like to start?'

Ending an online meeting

  • 'I'll send a summary of today's action points by end of day.'
  • 'The meeting recording will be available in the shared folder.'
  • 'Thank you all for joining. I'll end the meeting now.'

Useful expressions

Chairing a meeting

  • 'Let's get started. I'd like to welcome everyone to today's meeting.'
  • 'The aim of today's meeting is to …'
  • 'Could we have a quick round of introductions?'
  • 'We've covered all the items on the agenda. Thank you for your time.'

Contributing as a participant

  • 'I'd like to add something, if I may.'
  • 'Could I come back to an earlier point?'
  • 'Following on from what [name] said, …'
  • 'I'd like to raise a concern about …'

Useful vocabulary

Word / phraseMeaning
agendaA list of items to be discussed at a meeting.
chairperson / chairThe person who leads and manages a meeting.
action pointA specific task assigned to someone following a meeting, with a deadline.
minutesAn official written record of what was discussed and decided in a meeting.
attendeeA person who is present at a meeting or event.
quorumThe minimum number of people required to be present for a meeting to be valid.
adjournTo formally end or postpone a meeting.
Any Other Business (AOB)A standard agenda item at the end of a meeting for matters not listed on the main agenda.
muteTo turn off a microphone so that one's voice cannot be heard.
breakout roomA separate virtual space in an online meeting where small groups can discuss independently.
